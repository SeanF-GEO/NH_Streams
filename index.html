Documentation

Loading data: Select a state from the dropdown and click Load Streamflow to fetch the latest discharge readings for all USGS monitoring stations in that state. Alternatively, pan and zoom the map to your area of interest, then click Load by Map Extent to query within the current viewport. The Limit control caps how many station records are returned per request.

Reading the map: Each circle marker represents a USGS streamgage. The marker color indicates the current discharge magnitude according to the legend — a light-to-dark ramp from pale mint for very low flows through teals and blues, down to deep navy and indigo for the highest flows. Marker size also scales logarithmically with flow value, so larger circles mean higher flow. Darker markers have a lighter outline to remain visible on the dark basemap.

Station details: Click any marker to open a popup showing the station ID, current discharge value, measurement timestamp, data approval status, and any data qualifiers. From the popup you can pin the station for hydrograph comparison, or jump to the official USGS monitoring location page.

Comparing stations: Pin up to 10 stations by clicking the "Pin to Compare" button in each popup. Choose a lookback window (1–30 days) from the Days dropdown, then click Compare Pinned. The chart drawer will open at the bottom showing overlaid hydrographs for all pinned stations. Hover over the chart to see exact values at any point in time.

Stream lines: Toggle the NHD Stream Lines layer to overlay the USGS National Hydrography Dataset flowline network on the map. At wide zoom levels you'll see the small-scale nationwide network; as you zoom in, high-resolution flowlines appear with full detail down to headwater streams.

Source: All streamflow data comes from the USGS Water Data OGC API, a modernized, OGC-compliant interface to the National Water Information System (NWIS). This tool queries two collections:

  • latest-continuous — the single most recent observation for each time series, used for the map markers.
  • continuous — the full 15-minute interval time series, used when you pull historical data for the comparison chart.

Parameter: This viewer displays parameter code 00060, which is Discharge (cfs) — the volume of water flowing past the streamgage, measured in cubic feet per second. This is the most commonly reported hydrologic variable and the primary output of most USGS streamgage stations.

Approval status: Data may be marked Approved (reviewed and published) or Provisional (subject to revision). Provisional data has not yet completed the USGS review process. The USGS states that provisional data are released on the condition that neither the USGS nor the United States Government may be held liable for any damages resulting from its use.

Qualifiers: Some readings carry qualifier codes such as Ice (sensor affected by ice conditions), Eqp (equipment malfunction), or Estimated. These appear in the station popup when present.

Continuous vs. daily: Continuous ("instantaneous value" or "IV") data are typically recorded at 15-minute intervals by automated sensors. Daily values are statistical summaries (mean, min, max) computed from the continuous record. This tool uses the continuous record for maximum temporal resolution.

The stream line overlay comes from the USGS National Hydrography Dataset (NHD) map service. The NHD is a comprehensive geospatial dataset encoding surface water features including rivers, streams, canals, lakes, and reservoirs.

This overlay renders two scale-dependent layers from the service:

  • Small-scale flowlines (layer 4) — visible at wider zoom levels, showing the generalized national drainage network.
  • Large-scale flowlines (layer 6) — visible when zoomed in closer, showing high-resolution detail down to 1:24,000 scale including headwater streams, ditches, and canals.

The NHD is not a real-time dataset — it represents the mapped drainage network geometry. Flowline colors on this layer reflect NHD's own symbology (blue) and are not colored by current discharge values. To see which streams are being actively monitored, compare the flowlines to the streamgage markers.

Note: As of October 2023 the NHD was retired in favor of the 3D Hydrography Program (3DHP). The NHD data remains available and is still served through the map service used here, but future updates will come through 3DHP.

Markers are classified into six logarithmic bins based on instantaneous discharge in cubic feet per second (cfs):

  • Negative (< 0 cfs) — Indicates reverse flow, most commonly seen at tidally influenced stations where incoming tides push water upstream, or at sites affected by backwater conditions, dam operations, or wind-driven flow reversals. Shown in bright red to flag these unusual readings immediately.
  • Very Low (< 10 cfs) — Typical of small headwater streams, springs, or streams during drought/baseflow conditions. Pale mint, the lightest color in the ramp.
  • Low (10–100 cfs) — Common for small to mid-size streams under normal flow conditions. Muted teal.
  • Moderate (100–1,000 cfs) — Mid-range rivers or smaller rivers experiencing elevated flow from recent rainfall. Deep teal.
  • High (1,000–10,000 cfs) — Large rivers under normal conditions, or smaller rivers during significant storm events or snowmelt. Steel blue on the map.
  • Very High (10,000–100,000 cfs) — Major rivers like the Ohio or Columbia, or any river during flood events. Deep navy on the map.
  • Extreme (> 100,000 cfs) — The largest rivers in the country (Mississippi, Columbia during peak), or catastrophic flood events. Near-black indigo on the map.

These are fixed thresholds based on absolute discharge values. They do not reflect percentile or return-period statistics for individual stations — a "Very High" reading at one station may be normal for a large river but represent a rare flood on a small stream. For station-specific flood thresholds, consult the USGS Water Data for the Nation site.

This tool makes client-side HTTP requests directly to the USGS OGC API. No backend server is required — it runs entirely as a static HTML page, suitable for GitHub Pages deployment.

Endpoints used:

  • GET /collections/latest-continuous/items — fetches the most recent discharge observation per station, filtered by bounding box and parameter code.
  • GET /collections/continuous/items — fetches historical 15-minute interval time series for specific monitoring locations, filtered by datetime range.

Rate limits: The USGS API allows unauthenticated requests but imposes rate limits. For heavier use, sign up for a free API key and paste it into the API_KEY constant in the source code. Authenticated requests receive significantly higher rate limits.

Response format: Data is returned as GeoJSON (application/geo+json). Each feature includes a Point geometry (the station's lat/lon) and properties like monitoring_location_id, value, time, unit_of_measure, approval_status, and qualifier.

Pagination: The API returns up to 10,000 records per page. This tool follows next links automatically until the requested limit is reached. For historical queries, a 30-day window at 15-minute intervals produces ~2,880 records per station.

Libraries: Leaflet for mapping, esri-leaflet for NHD map service integration, Chart.js with the date-fns adapter for time-series charting.
